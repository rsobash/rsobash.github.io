I'm currently a post-doctoral research associate at the National Center for Atmospheric Research in Boulder, CO. I work within the data assimilation group in the Mesoscale and Microscale Meteorology (MMM) division.

Research Interests: Improving forecasts of extreme, high-impact weather, such as severe convection and its associated hazards (tornadoes, high winds, large hail). To do so, I primarily utilize novel data assimilation techniques and ensemble forecasting methods.

Research summary
I utilize a variety of novel tools and techniques in an effort to produce more accurate forecasts of severe weather hazards emanating from convective weather, such as severe thunderstorms. These events are frequently challenging to predict given their small size and short lifetimes compared to larger-scale phenomena such as hurricanes, fronts, or mid-latitude cyclones.
My interests in improving convective weather forecasts span many time-scales, from short forecast lead-times to assist in the weather warning process, to longer lead-times to improve the predictions of storms occurring hours or days into the future.
I rely heavily on ensemble data assimilation methods, combined with high-resolution numerical models, to improve convective weather forecasts. Ensemble systems are essential given the many uncertainties that exist with forecasting convective storms.
I'm also interesting in using ensemble systems to improve predictions of other high-impact weather, as well as ways to derive forecast guidance that incorporates uncertainty information from ensembles to improve decision-making.
